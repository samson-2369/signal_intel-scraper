Inputs Lie: Your System Trusts Signals It Shouldn't

Norris Cornell · 2024 · ICS / OT Security · Physical-Layer
Every control loop starts before the PLC.

Somewhere upstream, a sensor is measuring something real — temperature, pressure, flow, position. That measurement becomes a signal. That signal becomes a value. That value becomes a decision. And nowhere in that chain does the system ask: is this signal telling the truth?

This is not a theoretical vulnerability. It is the structural condition of industrial control systems as they exist today — in water treatment plants, power substations, oil pipelines, and manufacturing floors. The control loop is designed to act on inputs. The security architecture is designed to protect the network. And the gap between those two things is where sophisticated adversaries are operating right now.

The "Inputs Lie" framework starts here: with the recognition that the real starting point of every industrial process is not software, not firmware, not protocol — it is a physical signal that the system has decided to trust unconditionally.

The Control Loop Starts Before You Think It Does

Most ICS security practitioners think about the attack surface in terms of networks, protocols, and devices. Modbus is unauthenticated. DNP3 has known weaknesses. The HMI is exposed. These are real problems. But they are not the beginning of the chain — they are somewhere in the middle.

The actual beginning looks like this:

// INDUSTRIAL CONTROL LOOP — SIGNAL TRUST CHAIN
01
Physical world event — a valve opens, a tank fills, a motor spins ⚡ No security consideration exists at this layer
02
Sensor measurement — transducer converts physical state to electrical signal ⚡ Signal integrity assumed, not verified
03
Signal transmission — analog or digital signal travels over wiring or RF ⚡ Transmission path is typically unmonitored
04
Input module / RTU — signal digitized and passed to PLC ⚡ Value accepted as ground truth
05
PLC logic evaluation — deterministic program executes based on received value ⚡ Logic is sound. The premise it operates on may not be.
06
Physical actuation — output commands change state of the real world ⚡ Consequence delivered. No rollback possible.

The security community spends most of its attention on steps four and five — the digital domain. But steps one through three are where an adversary with patience, resources, and physical access can introduce manipulated data that travels faithfully through every subsequent layer, never triggering a single alert.

"The PLC is doing exactly what it was designed to do. The problem is what it was designed to do with."

Why Digital Logic Cannot Verify Physical Truth

There is a seductive assumption embedded in how most engineers think about PLCs: because the logic is deterministic, the output is reliable. If the program is correct, the system behaves correctly. This is true in isolation. It is false in an adversarial environment.

Deterministic logic operates on the premise that its inputs accurately represent the physical world. That premise is never verified. It cannot be verified — at least not from within the control system itself. The PLC has no independent ground truth. It knows only what its sensors tell it, processed through whatever signal conditioning exists in the input modules.

This creates a structural vulnerability: an adversary who controls what the sensor reports controls what the PLC decides.

They do not need to compromise the PLC. They do not need to bypass the firewall. They do not need to exploit a protocol vulnerability. They need to make the sensor lie — consistently, plausibly, within operational parameters — and the deterministic logic does the rest for them.

The Gap Exists Because of How Legacy Systems Were Built

It is worth being precise about why this gap exists, because understanding its origin is necessary to understand why it persists.

Industrial control systems were not designed for adversarial environments. They were designed for reliability and uptime in environments where the threat model was component failure, not intentional manipulation. Sensor validation, where it existed at all, was designed to detect hardware faults — a wire breaking, a sensor going out of range, a value stuck at zero. It was not designed to detect a sophisticated adversary deliberately keeping a value within plausible bounds while causing gradual drift toward a dangerous state.

The difference between a hardware fault and a sophisticated signal attack is intentionality. One produces values that look broken. The other produces values that look normal — until the process crosses a threshold that cannot be recovered.

The legacy of this design approach is infrastructure that was installed decades ago, will remain in service for decades more, and cannot be replaced wholesale without disrupting the processes it controls. Patching this gap requires adding validation where none was designed to exist — which is architecturally difficult and operationally risky in environments where downtime has immediate physical consequences.

What Signal-Layer Attacks Look Like

Because signal-layer manipulation is designed to stay below detection thresholds, it does not produce the obvious indicators that network-based attacks do. There are no error logs. No anomalous connections. No authentication failures. What it does produce are subtle fingerprints that, in hindsight, were always present.

FINGERPRINT · 01

Gradual Drift

Sensor values move incrementally over hours or days — each individual reading within plausible bounds, but collectively representing a trajectory the process should never follow.

FINGERPRINT · 02

Cross-Sensor Disagreement

Redundant sensors measuring the same physical parameter begin diverging without a physical explanation. One is measuring reality. The other may not be.

FINGERPRINT · 03

Timing Anomalies

Control sequences that should be synchronized begin falling out of phase. Events that should trigger together begin decoupling — particularly relevant when GNSS timing is involved.

FINGERPRINT · 04

Implausible Quiescence

A sensor that should vary with process conditions begins reporting an unusually stable value. Stability is normally desirable — but artificially stable signals are a red flag.

None of these individually are definitive. Sensors drift. Redundant sensors disagree for legitimate reasons. This is precisely what makes signal-layer attacks effective against conventional detection: the same signatures appear in normal equipment aging and in deliberate manipulation. Distinguishing between them requires cross-sensor correlation, process physics modeling, and pattern analysis over time — capabilities that most OT environments simply do not have deployed today.

The Framework Gap: Where Current Security Falls Short

MITRE ATT&CK for ICS represents the most mature public framework for mapping adversary behavior in industrial environments. It is useful, important, and incomplete. What it describes well is behavior at and above the device layer — techniques like "Modify Control Logic," "Block Reporting Message," and "Spoof Reporting Message." What it addresses less directly is the physical-layer manipulation that precedes all of that.

SECURITY LAYER WHAT IT COVERS WHAT IT MISSES
Network Security Unauthorized connections, protocol anomalies, lateral movement Attacks that never touch the network
Endpoint Security Malware, unauthorized software, configuration drift Compromised inputs that make clean endpoints do bad things
Protocol Monitoring Anomalous commands, unauthorized writes, message injection Legitimate commands issued in response to false sensor data
SIEM / SOAR Log aggregation, alert correlation, incident response Physical-layer events that produce no logs
Process Monitoring Known alarm setpoints, operator-defined thresholds Sub-threshold manipulation designed to stay within alarm bounds

The gap is not a failure of any of these tools. Each does what it was designed to do. The gap exists because the threat model that justified their design did not include adversaries operating at the physical layer. That threat model is no longer adequate.

What "Inputs Lie" Requires of Defenders

Closing this gap is not a product purchase. It is an architectural and analytical posture — a deliberate decision to treat external signals as untrusted until they can be corroborated. In practice, this means building defenses that operate at and below the device layer.

PRINCIPLE 01

Threat-Model the Signal Chain, Not Just the Network

Every external input to a control system — sensor readings, GPS timing, external reference signals — should be evaluated for how it could be manipulated, by whom, and to what effect. Start the threat model before the PLC.

PRINCIPLE 02

Require Cross-Sensor Corroboration for Safety-Critical Values

No single sensor should be the sole input to a safety-critical decision. Redundant sensing with cross-correlation and physical-model validation creates the minimum viable signal integrity posture. Disagreement between sensors is an alert condition, not a nuisance.

PRINCIPLE 03

Monitor Rate-of-Change, Not Just Threshold

Alarm setpoints are designed to catch hardware faults. They are not designed to catch gradual, deliberate drift. Process monitoring systems should track rate-of-change trends alongside absolute values — the trajectory of a reading over time can be more informative than the reading itself.

PRINCIPLE 04

Treat GNSS Timing as an External Input That Can Lie

GPS-derived timing is deeply embedded in grid synchronization, substation protection, and SCADA timestamping. It is also an RF signal broadcast from space that can be spoofed or jammed. Systems that depend on GPS timing for safety-critical functions should have authenticated backup timing sources.

PRINCIPLE 05

Document Signal Assumptions Explicitly

Most control system documentation specifies what signals are used. Very few document what those signals are assumed to represent, over what range they are considered valid, and what behavior should occur if they are inconsistent with corroborating measurements. Making trust assumptions explicit is the first step to auditing them.

If your threat model begins at the network perimeter, you are starting too late. The adversary who understands this will always have a layer of access you are not watching.

Why This Is Hard to Fix

The "Inputs Lie" problem does not have a straightforward remediation path, and it would be dishonest to suggest otherwise. Several realities complicate any practical defensive response:

  • Legacy infrastructure cannot be easily modified. Sensor wiring, input modules, and RTU configurations were installed to run processes, not to implement security architectures. Retrofitting cross-validation logic into systems that were not designed for it requires engineering work that operations teams are rarely resourced to undertake.
  • Process physics expertise is required. Distinguishing sensor manipulation from normal process variation requires understanding what the process should look like under normal operating conditions — knowledge that sits with process engineers, not security analysts. Effective defense at this layer requires a collaboration between disciplines that rarely talk to each other.
  • The attacker has time. A patient adversary can conduct reconnaissance of normal operating patterns over weeks or months before manipulating signals. They know what "normal" looks like. Any detection system that relies on detecting deviation from normal must also account for an attacker who spent time learning what normal is.
  • There is no patch for physics. Network vulnerabilities can be patched. Protocol weaknesses can be mitigated. Signal-layer vulnerabilities require physical changes — redundant sensing, shielding, authenticated timing references — that have cost, complexity, and operational risk associated with them.

None of this means defense is impossible. It means defense is different than it is for conventional IT security — more deliberate, more specialized, more expensive, and more tightly coupled to the specific processes being protected.

The most dangerous assumption in ICS security is that a signal is telling the truth because it arrived within range.

Threat-model your signals. Validate across sensors. Monitor trajectory, not just threshold. Start the security model before the PLC.
