Cyber Hygiene 101

What NIST Says About Keeping Your Digital Footprint Clean

Norris Cornell · Fundamentals · NIST SP 800-53 · IAM / Hygiene

Cyber hygiene is your digital lifeline. It's the daily grind that keeps your footprint clean and attackers at bay: strong passwords, timely updates, and locked-down configurations. Skip it, and you're setting yourself up for an expensive lesson.

I've spent years working in SIEM monitoring, vulnerability management, and security hardening — triaging Splunk logs, locking down financial systems, and following NIST best practices. One thing is clear across all of it: cyber hygiene isn't optional — it's survival.

MGM's $100M Cyber Hygiene Lesson

$100M+ MGM RESORTS BREACH · SEPTEMBER 2023
10 days OUTAGE DURATION
29 PROPERTIES AFFECTED
30M+ RECORDS EXPOSED
6+ TB DATA STOLEN

On September 8, 2023, ALPHV/BlackCat and Scattered Spider launched a ransomware attack across 29 MGM properties — Bellagio, MGM Grand, and more. ATMs crashed, slot machines failed, and digital room keys stopped working. The cause wasn't a sophisticated zero-day.

ATTACK CHAIN · HOW THREE STEPS COST $100M

How It Happened

01

Social Engineering — The Weakest Link. An attacker scraped an MGM employee's LinkedIn profile, called IT support, and social-engineered their way into an MFA reset. Ten minutes of conversation unlocked the door.

02

Poor Identity Verification. With the MFA reset in hand, the attacker gained admin access to Okta and Azure Active Directory — then exfiltrated over 6 terabytes of data including SSNs, passport numbers, and personal information for millions of customers.

03

Weak MFA and No Real-Time Monitoring. No advanced authentication verification. No anomaly detection that flagged a help desk reset being followed immediately by admin-level access to identity systems. A textbook cyber hygiene failure at every layer.

MGM refused to pay the ransom. The damage was done anyway: $100M in lost revenue, ongoing litigation, and tens of millions of customer records exposed.

MGM didn't fall to a nation-state exploit or a zero-day vulnerability. They fell to a phone call, a LinkedIn search, and an IT help desk that skipped identity verification. Every item in this post would have stopped it.

01 MFA, Strong Passwords & Password Managers NIST SP 800-53: IA-2, AC-7

MFA is the single highest-impact, lowest-cost control in the hygiene stack. If MGM had enforced strict MFA policies with identity verification requirements at the help desk — as NIST IA-2 mandates — this breach does not happen. One control. One hundred million dollars saved.

// FOR BUSINESSES

Enforce company-wide MFA and train your help desk to verify every identity before any account change — especially MFA resets. That's where MGM failed. A 10-minute phone call should not unlock admin access to your identity platform.

// FOR INDIVIDUALS

Ditch weak passwords. Use passphrases (e.g., Ph1llyC15A!). Enable MFA on everything — email, banking, work accounts. No exceptions.

Use a password manager — Bitwarden or 1Password are solid choices. NIST recommends password managers specifically to eliminate password reuse and reduce fatigue. One unique, complex password per account. The manager remembers them so you don't have to.

FIELD NOTE

I've seen one-time codes stop ransomware cold. Use them. MFA is not a suggestion for sophisticated users — it's table stakes for everyone.

02 Patch & Update Everything NIST SP 800-53: SI-2

Unpatched systems are not a technical problem — they're a policy failure. Every day a known vulnerability sits unpatched is a day an attacker can exploit it. MGM's 10-day operational outage strongly suggests poor patching discipline contributed to the blast radius of the breach.

// FOR BUSINESSES

Schedule monthly patch cycles. Automate vulnerability scanning with tools like Nessus. Patch before attackers exploit the gap — not after the incident report is written.

// FOR INDIVIDUALS

When iOS or Windows says there's an update, run it. Those prompts aren't nagging — they're closing zero-days that researchers found and attackers are already testing.

FIELD NOTE

Unpatched endpoints are a straight path to breach. I've seen vulnerability scans flag critical CVEs on systems that hadn't been touched in 18 months — running software with public exploit code available.

03 Secure Configurations & Endpoint Protection NIST SP 800-53: CM-6, SC-7

MGM's admin access fell in ten minutes. That suggests default settings, overly permissive role-based access controls, or open ports that should have been closed. Misconfigurations are not edge cases — they are the most consistently exploited vulnerability class in enterprise environments.

// FOR BUSINESSES

Scan configurations quarterly. Lock down admin privileges using least-privilege access. Close ports that aren't needed. Every open port is an attack surface — close what you don't use.

// FOR INDIVIDUALS

Secure your home Wi-Fi — use WPA3, change the default router password, and disable services you don't use. Your router is the perimeter of your home network. Treat it like one.

FIELD NOTE

I've run Nessus scans that surfaced open ports and misconfigurations that could have ended in a breach — on systems that hadn't been reviewed since initial deployment. Quarterly scans are not optional.

Cyber Hygiene: It's Not Optional

MGM is proof that basic cyber hygiene failures cost millions — and that the failures are almost always preventable. These controls are not advanced. They're not expensive. They're just consistently ignored until they aren't.

// BUSINESSES
  • Train your help desk — social engineering is the most common entry point
  • Roll out MFA company-wide — one weak account is all it takes
  • Patch on a schedule — monthly, no exceptions
  • Scan and lock down configurations — misconfigs are hacker gold
  • Audit RBAC — no one should have more access than they need
// INDIVIDUALS
  • Stop using weak passwords — use passphrases and MFA everywhere
  • Use a password manager — unique passwords for every account
  • Update weekly — patches close holes attackers are actively exploiting
  • Lock your router — WPA3, changed defaults, unused services off
  • Audit your LinkedIn — oversharing helps attackers build your profile

None of this is hard. All of it is skipped constantly. The gap between knowing and doing is where breaches happen.
