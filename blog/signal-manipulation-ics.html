Signal Manipulation in Industrial Control Systems: Why Traditional Security Fails at the Physics Layer

// NOTE ON RESPONSIBLE DISCLOSURE

All signal manipulation techniques discussed in this article are drawn from publicly available academic research, industry standards, and government security guidance. The goal is to help defenders understand threats they may not have considered — not to provide attack tutorials.

When Harmonics Become Attack Vectors

Industrial control systems are built on an assumption: that input signals follow predictable physical laws. A 4-20mA current loop should vary smoothly. A 60Hz AC waveform should be sinusoidal. A sensor reporting temperature should change gradually, not instantaneously.

But physics also gives us harmonics — integer multiples of fundamental frequencies that naturally occur in electrical systems. A 60Hz power signal generates harmonics at 120Hz, 180Hz, 240Hz, and so on. In normal operation, engineers design systems to filter out these harmonics because they represent noise or inefficiency.

Here's the security problem: Most control systems validate the fundamental frequency but don't verify harmonic content. They ask "is this a 60Hz signal?" without asking "is this a clean 60Hz signal?"

An adversary with physical or RF access doesn't need to compromise authentication systems or break encryption. They can inject harmonic content that causes sensors to misreport values by pushing analog-to-digital converters outside their expected operating range. This triggers protective relays prematurely or bypasses signal validation entirely — because the fundamental frequency remains correct.

The Detection Challenge: Moving Beyond Fourier Analysis

If you have an engineering background, your first instinct is likely "Use Fourier analysis." The mathematics are sound — a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) decomposes any signal into its constituent frequencies, revealing malicious harmonics.

In theory, you could alarm when the frequency spectrum deviates. But operational reality makes this difficult. Standard Fourier analysis tells you what frequencies are present, but not precisely when they occurred — meaning short burst attacks can be missed. Furthermore, legacy SCADA infrastructure simply lacks the computational power to perform real-time FFT on thousands of sensor inputs.

We have spent two decades building firewalls and authentication systems to secure Modbus and DNP3 packets, while leaving the analog world — the physics itself — completely unmonitored.

Amplitude Manipulation: Making Sensors Lie

The simplest signal attack is often the most effective: change the amplitude, and the system reads a different value.

A 4-20mA current loop is the workhorse of industrial instrumentation. The current value directly represents the measured parameter — 4mA might represent 0 PSI, and 20mA represents 100 PSI. The system reads the current and trusts that it accurately reflects reality.

The vulnerability: If an attacker can inject or attenuate current on that loop, they can make the system believe anything they want. They can report 8mA when actual pressure is at 18mA. Suddenly, a critically high-pressure condition appears safe on the operator's HMI. The SCADA system logs "everything fine" while physical equipment fails.

Everything looks normal — until it isn't.

This isn't exotic — it's basic circuit manipulation. But it is devastatingly effective because most systems have no way to validate that the electrical signal matches physical reality.

Timing Attacks: When Nanoseconds Matter

Power grids rely on synchrophasors (PMUs) to monitor grid stability across vast distances. These devices must timestamp measurements with microsecond precision to detect phase angle differences. If an attacker can manipulate the timing reference, they can make a destabilizing event invisible.

Many critical systems use GPS as their timing source because it is convenient and free. But GPS signals are extraordinarily weak — about -130 dBm, roughly equivalent to viewing a 25-watt light bulb from 10,000 miles away. A spoofer transmitting from a parking lot can easily overpower the authentic signal.

The system receives the spoofed signal. The structure looks correct. The satellite IDs are valid. But the timing is shifted. Digital validation passes — and the attack succeeds because the system trusts the "verified" input.

Modulation Attacks: Hiding in the Noise

Radio frequency systems use modulation to encode information. Systems expect this modulation to follow specific patterns. However, in shared spectrum environments like Industrial Wireless (ISA100.11a or WirelessHART), receivers are designed to be robust rather than suspicious.

An attacker can embed malicious modulation within legitimate signals or inject noise that looks like standard environmental interference. To a standard receiver trying to maintain connectivity, this might look like a dropped packet or a retransmission request. In reality, it effectively creates a denial-of-service condition for specific sensors — without ever triggering an intrusion alert.

Where Traditional Security Controls Fail

We must face a hard truth: the security controls we have spent billions deploying — segmentation, encryption, and IDS — do not solve physics-layer problems.

1. Network Segmentation: Protecting the Wrong Boundary

The Purdue Model relies on firewalls to separate business networks from control networks. This works for network-based threats. But signal manipulation doesn't cross network boundaries — it bypasses them entirely.

A pressure transmitter connects to a PLC via an analog copper cable. There are no packets to filter, no IP addresses to whitelist, and no TCP handshakes to inspect. You cannot firewall a wire.

2. Authentication: The "Verified Source" Trap

Authentication answers the question: "Is this message from an authorized source?" It does not answer: "Is the information in this message accurate?"

If an attacker manipulates the analog signal feeding a sensor, the sensor faithfully reports the false value. The sensor then authenticates to the SCADA system and transmits the lie. The SCADA system accepts it because it trusts the authenticated source. Authentication succeeded; therefore, the attack succeeded.

3. Encryption: Protecting Data That's Already Wrong

Encryption protects the integrity of data while it is being transmitted digitally. It does not protect the accuracy of data before it enters the digital domain. Consider the path of a manipulated temperature reading:

01
Physical Reality — Tank is 180°F.
02
Attack — Attacker injects voltage offset.
03
Sensor Reading — Circuit interprets input as 150°F.
04
A/D Conversion — 150°F becomes a digital value.
05
Encryption — The false value (150°F) is securely encrypted via TLS.
06
Processing — SCADA logs 150°F.

Encryption protected the transmission perfectly. But the fundamental input was false from the moment it entered the electrical domain. We have built armored transport for cargo that was swapped before it was ever loaded.

4. Intrusion Detection: Blind to Physics

IDS tools look for anomalies in how devices communicate — malformed packets, unauthorized commands, or scanning behavior. During a signal attack, the protocol usage is correct. The sample rate is standard. The data values are within plausible ranges.

Everything looks normal — until it isn't. The IDS is looking for protocol anomalies, not anomalies in the physics of what is being communicated.

What Defenders Actually Need

If we cannot rely on firewalls or encryption to solve this, what is the solution? We need to move from "Digital Trust" to "Physical Verification."

  • Process Validity Checks Program PLCs with logic that rejects physical impossibilities. If a water temperature reading jumps from 60°F to 180°F in 10 milliseconds, that violates the laws of thermodynamics. The PLC should flag this as an error — regardless of whether the sensor is "authenticated."
  • Signal Integrity Monitoring We don't need full FFT on every sensor, but we do need anomaly detection on signal characteristics. Monitor rise times, noise floors, and unexpected frequency content.
  • Multi-Sensor Correlation Never trust a single input for a critical decision. If three independent sensors usually correlate, and one suddenly diverges, the system should treat it as untrusted.

The Fundamental Assumption Gap

All traditional security controls share a common assumption: that inputs from physical sensors represent ground truth.

This isn't a failure of engineering — it's a consequence of building security controls for digital systems while treating the analog-to-digital boundary as out-of-scope. IT security operates in a domain where inputs are discrete and defined. OT security operates in a domain where inputs are interpretations of continuous physical phenomena.

If your security program doesn't account for this, you have a blind spot at the most fundamental layer of your system.
