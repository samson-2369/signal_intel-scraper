Asset Visibility in OT Environments: Why You Can't Defend What You Don't See

Norris Cornell · ICS/OT Security · Asset Management · Critical Infrastructure
Visibility isn't nice to have in OT security. It's non-negotiable.

If you don't know what assets exist on your industrial network, you can't protect them. You can't patch them. You can't monitor their behavior. And you can't detect when something goes wrong.

Asset visibility is the bedrock of any effective ICS cybersecurity strategy. Yet in many critical infrastructure environments, it remains one of the most overlooked and underdeveloped components of cyber hygiene. This post explores why asset visibility is so challenging in OT, how to implement it safely, and the tools and frameworks that can help.

The Visibility Gap in Industrial Networks

Traditional IT environments typically have robust visibility. From asset management platforms to endpoint detection agents, most organizations have some degree of insight into what's on their networks. OT environments are a different story.

Industrial networks often consist of decades-old systems, proprietary protocols, and "flat" architectures with little to no segmentation. Many assets — like programmable logic controllers (PLCs), human-machine interfaces (HMIs), and data historians — were never designed with cybersecurity in mind. They often lack logging, encryption, and even basic authentication mechanisms.

Why Asset Discovery Is Difficult in OT

Active Scanning Is Risky

Conventional tools like Nmap or Nessus can crash fragile devices. The same probe that returns a banner on an IT server can cause a PLC to fault or reboot.

Proprietary Protocols

Vendors use unique communication standards — Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP, PROFINET — making deep inspection without specialized parsers difficult or impossible.

Air-Gap Assumptions

Many environments still operate under the illusion of isolation. "Air-gapped" systems are often not as separated as assumed — and even true air gaps don't eliminate the need for asset awareness.

Lack of Inventory Discipline

New devices get added without documentation or centralized oversight. A technician installs a device in the field; it never makes it into the asset register.

Case Study: Colonial Pipeline

CASE STUDY · 2021 — COLONIAL PIPELINE RANSOMWARE ATTACK

What Poor Visibility Looks Like at Scale

The Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack is a high-profile example of how dangerous poor asset visibility can be. Attackers gained access to Colonial Pipeline's IT network using a compromised VPN password for an unused account that lacked multifactor authentication.

Although the ransomware did not spread into OT systems, the company made the decision to shut down its entire pipeline operation out of caution and uncertainty — highlighting a major lack of confidence in their visibility and segmentation between IT and OT networks.

The shutdown led to widespread fuel shortages and price spikes along the East Coast, demonstrating how a cyber incident in the business network can cascade into massive real-world consequences when visibility is incomplete.

Key lesson: Without full awareness of what systems are connected and how they communicate, organizations in critical infrastructure may be left in the dark when it matters most — even when the OT systems themselves weren't directly compromised.

Passive Discovery: The Gold Standard for OT

To gain visibility without disrupting operations, passive monitoring is the right approach. Instead of probing devices directly, these tools listen to network traffic, identify devices and protocols in use, and build a picture of the environment over time.

How Passive Discovery Works

01

Configure SPAN ports, TAPs, or mirror ports on industrial switches to copy traffic to a dedicated collection interface without touching endpoint devices.

02

Feed mirrored traffic to passive sensors or a dedicated collection server running your chosen analysis tools.

03

Tools parse traffic and fingerprint devices by IP address, MAC address, vendor, protocol in use, and firmware version — building an asset picture without touching endpoints.

04

Over time, the tool builds a dynamic, evolving asset inventory that reflects the actual state of the network rather than documentation that may be years out of date.

Passive discovery is the only safe approach for most OT environments. Read the traffic — don't probe the devices. The network will tell you everything you need to know.

Building and Maintaining an OT Asset Inventory

Discovery is just the start. To be useful for cyber defense, visibility must translate into a structured and up-to-date asset inventory. The inventory is only valuable if it's accurate, current, and actionable.

◈

Tag criticality. Classify assets based on their impact on safety, operations, or business continuity. Not every device carries equal risk — prioritize your protective effort accordingly.

◈

Track firmware and software versions. Essential for identifying vulnerable components when advisories are published. A PLC running five-year-old firmware you don't know about is a silent liability.

◈

Use CMDBs or EAM systems. Centralize your data so that it feeds into risk management and SOC workflows. Passive discovery output should flow into your inventory automatically, not require manual transcription.

◈

Automate updates where possible. Sync passive discovery data into your inventory system regularly. A static inventory is a false confidence. The network changes; your inventory must change with it.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) emphasizes this in the Identify function — the first and foundational step. You can't protect, detect, respond, or recover without first knowing what you have.

Detecting Rogue and Undocumented Devices

Even with a solid asset inventory, you must remain alert for changes — especially unexpected or unauthorized ones. Threat actors may introduce devices into your environment that never appear in documentation.

ADVERSARY TACTICS — UNAUTHORIZED DEVICE INTRODUCTION
ROGUE ENGINEERING LAPTOPS Introduced by contractors or attackers with physical access; used to connect directly to engineering networks.
UNMANAGED NETWORK PORTS Physical ports in equipment rooms, substations, or remote sites that are unplugged but reachable — easy insertion points.
COVERT RELAY DEVICES Small single-board computers (e.g., Raspberry Pi) installed inline or on a tap to intercept or relay traffic without disrupting the connection.
MITRE ATT&CK FOR ICS T0809: Rogue Master — adversary device poses as a legitimate master device to issue unauthorized commands to field devices.

Detection Techniques

Establish a communication baseline. Know what normal traffic looks like — which devices talk to which, what protocols they use, and what volumes are expected. Deviation from baseline is your first signal.

Alert on new MAC addresses. Any device that appears on the network with a previously unseen MAC address should trigger an investigation, not a ticket. In OT environments, new devices are rare and should always be expected.

Monitor for unusual protocol use or port scans. Passive tools should flag any host attempting to probe or query devices it doesn't normally communicate with — a strong indicator of reconnaissance or lateral movement.

Ingest logs into a SIEM. Platforms like Splunk or Elastic can correlate passive discovery alerts, firewall logs, and historian data to surface anomalies that no single tool would catch in isolation.

By continuously monitoring for deviation, you turn passive discovery from a one-time inventory exercise into a proactive defense mechanism — one that generates alerts when the environment changes in unexpected ways.

Visibility Is the First Step Toward Control

The Foundation Everything Else Is Built On

In ICS and OT environments, asset visibility isn't a one-time project — it's a continuous process that forms the foundation of every other security initiative. Without it, segmentation is blind, threat detection is unreliable, and incident response is incomplete.

If your organization is still operating in the dark, start with passive discovery. Build an inventory. Map criticality. Monitor for change. These are not glamorous capabilities — but they are the ones that determine whether every other investment in OT security actually works.

You can't defend what you don't see — but once you do see it, you can take meaningful, risk-based action.
